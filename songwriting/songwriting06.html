The Art and Skill of Writing a Song

6. Developing the idea.

Engaging the Left Brain

We now have enough material, whether it be from our cluster exercises or our journal, to start to organise into a song. Now might be a good time to pick up your guitar or sit down to your keyboard. Remember, we've put a certain amount of time aside to write a song and that's what we're going to do. We're going to finish this in one sitting. (It might not end up being the best song in the world but we're practising songwriting and learning to apply new techniques.) This is where we need our left brain to push on through and not let our imagination run away with us - we've done that bit.

Keys to Left Brain Thinking

  • Minimise distractions.
  • For the excessively right-brained among us, coffee or caffeinated drinks may help.
  • Work to a deadline.
  • Make decisions quickly, move on.

If you’re narrating a story or event, don’t over-describe. Use key words or concepts as quick steppingstones. Keep the same pace of narrative: if you’re writing about your wife and dog leaving you in one verse, don’t spend two lines describing the empty dog kennel. Remember too that the listener doesn’t have to understand what you mean, what they think it means may be even better!

There’s nothing to say that you have to write a song first verse first. If it’s not immediately obvious what comes next, try something that might come before. For example, make your first verse your second verse and write a new first one.

Don’t “contrive” your rhyme. If it doesn’t sound natural, don’t use it just because it rhymes (left brain will want a good rhyme, and want to explain why it’s a good rhyme). Similarly, don’t feel the melody has to be exactly the same in the second verse as in the first if the words don’t allow for it.

Sing it the way you say it. Be careful of putting the emPHAsis on the wrong sylLAble (not never, just hardly ever). Also, make sure the key word is in the right beat of the bar where it gets the proper focus.

Move on. Don’t get bogged down in bits you think could be better. We are going to have some work done, some tangible results by the end of our songwriting session even if it’s not perfect. Remember: If you want to write one good song, be prepared to write ten bad ones.
